Key insight for UVA: Because UVA's annual cost of attendance (~$78K) exceeds the OBBB's $50K/year federal cap by ~$28K each year, students must finance ~$149K of their medical education through private loans at higher interest rates (est. 11% vs. 7.94% federal). Even though UVA is the most affordable program among the six public schools in this analysis, OBBB still adds $26,310 in total lifetime borrowing costs and raises the monthly payment by $220/month. During a 3–7 year residency, when most physicians cannot afford standard repayment, additional interest capitalization would further widen this gap.

Model assumptions: Tuition + fees from AAMC "Tuition and Student Fees Report" (AY 2022-23, resident/in-state). Living expenses: $27,000/year (AAMC standard estimate for misc. living, health insurance, supplies). Current law rates: Graduate Unsubsidized Stafford = 7.94%; Graduate PLUS = 8.94% (AY 2025-26 rates); weighted average based on Stafford ($20,500/yr) + PLUS (remaining COA). OBBB: Federal loans capped at $50,000/year professional; aggregate $200,000 (all federal); assumed $37,000 prior undergraduate federal debt (AAMC median), leaving $163,000 available for medical school. Remaining COA above federal cap financed via private loans at 11.0% (industry average). All loans assumed to accrue interest during 4 years of medical training (no payments made); balance at graduation repaid over 10 years (standard plan). Does not account for residency deferment, income-driven repayment, or institutional grant aid. Private loan availability and rates may vary. Source: AAMC Tuition and Student Fees Report 2013–2026; AAMC Physician Education Debt Report; Federal Student Aid (studentaid.gov); H.R. 1 / One Big Beautiful Bill Act (signed 2025).
